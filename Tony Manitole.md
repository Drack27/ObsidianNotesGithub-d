# Tony Manitole

**Age:** Mid 50s
**Residence:** A small house behind [[Tony's Diner]].
**Occupation:** Owner and operator of Tony's Diner.

---

## Description

Tony Manitole is the heart of Roselake's social life — or what passes for it. He's a broad-shouldered, balding man with a warm smile, a loud laugh, and the kind of easy friendliness that makes everyone who walks into his diner feel like a regular. He has owned and operated Tony's Diner for over twenty years, and in a town with nowhere else to go, the diner has become the default gathering place for anyone who wants company.

Tony has a particular fondness for the younger crowd in Roselake. He lets high school kids hang around the diner after hours, gives them free coffee when they look like they need it, and listens to their problems with the patience of a man who never had kids of his own. The teenagers of Roselake think of Tony as an uncle figure — someone who's on their side, no questions asked.

---

## Relationship with Thomas McMullin

Tony was one of the very few people who managed to hold regular conversations with Thomas McMullin. Thomas would come into the diner, sit at the counter, and talk about conspiracy theories — always framing them as an intellectual interest, never a belief. Tony listened, asked questions, and kept Thomas's coffee full. He didn't believe the theories, but he liked the company.

Tony noticed when Thomas stopped coming in. He hasn't raised an alarm yet — Thomas was always erratic in his habits — but if someone asks, Tony will mention that he hasn't seen Thomas in a few days and that's unusual, even for him.

Tony is also a reliable source of town gossip. He hears everything that passes through the diner — complaints about the Marsh house, worries about the Hutchins family's "ghost," talk about the Peabody house disappearances, observations about who's been acting strange lately. He doesn't put the pieces together the way Thomas did, but he has all the pieces.

---

## Personality

Tony is gregarious, generous, and fundamentally decent. He is not a complicated man. He runs a diner, he likes people, and he worries about his town. The increasing strangeness in Roselake bothers him, but he deals with it the way he deals with everything — by keeping the doors open, keeping the coffee hot, and making sure people have somewhere to go.
