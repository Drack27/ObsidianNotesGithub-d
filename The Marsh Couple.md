# Reuben & Clara Marsh

**Ages:** Late 50s
**Residence:** One of the older houses on the south end of town, built in the early 1900s.
**Case Connection:** [[The Weeping House]]

---

## Background

Reuben and Clara Marsh have lived in their home for over twenty years. They are a deeply superstitious couple — on the older end of people who joined the new age movement in the 1960s, and their home reflects decades of spiritual seeking.

## The House

Walking into the Marsh home is like walking into a timeline of American spiritual trends:

- **Tarot cards** are scattered on end tables and dressers — Clara does readings, though she'd be the first to admit she's not very good at them.
- A **seance room** occupies what was once a guest bedroom. The table is covered in a dark velvet cloth, and there are candles that have been burned down to nubs and re-burned dozens of times.
- **Crystals** of every size and color are placed throughout the house — on windowsills, bookshelves, nightstands. Amethyst clusters, quartz points, and polished stones that Clara insists have healing properties.
- **Books about chakra alignment**, energy healing, aura reading, and astral projection fill a dedicated bookshelf in the living room.
- More recently — and this is the part that stands out — a **significant amount of Catholic paraphernalia** has appeared in the house. Crosses on nearly every wall. A crucifix above the front door. Books on demonology and spiritual warfare. A rosary draped over the bedroom mirror. Holy water in a small dish by the entryway.

The shift from crystals and chakras to crosses and demonology has been recent and sudden. It tells the story of two people who spent their lives believing in the gentle supernatural — energy, spirits, the universe — and have recently come to believe they are dealing with something far darker and more malevolent than they ever imagined.

## Personalities

**Reuben** is the more frustrated of the two. He's a practical man by nature who was always somewhat dragged along by Clara's spiritual interests, but he's now fully on board — not because he's found faith, but because he's terrified. He is angry that no one is taking their situation seriously. He has been telling anyone at the diner, the church, and the general store who will listen about what's happening in their home.

**Clara** is the more frightened. She has always been the spiritual one in the relationship, and she takes the signs in their home very seriously. She is the one who describes the water from the taps as "blood." She is the one who has been buying the crosses and the demonology books. She is the one who insisted they contact Thomas McMullin.

## Their Conviction

The Marshes are nervous, hesitant, but deeply convicted in their belief that whatever is infesting their home is **not a departed human soul, but something demonic and hellish in nature.** They point to the sulfurous smell as their primary evidence — everyone knows that sulfur means the Devil, they say. The blood from the taps. The pounding in the walls. The way the house feels heavy and oppressive, like something thick and hateful is sitting in the walls.

They anxiously latch onto this interpretation. It gives shape to their fear. A demon is terrifying, but at least it's something you can name, something the Church has fought before. The alternative — that they don't know what it is — is worse.

When Thomas McMullin checked the roof and found no source for the ceiling leak, the Marshes latched onto this as well. If there's no leak, then the stain on the ceiling can't be water. And if it's not water, then what is the house sweating? Thomas, who is not a plumber, simply went along with their reasoning at the time.

## Connection to Agatha Graves

Clara Marsh has been buying spiritual protective goods — crosses and rosaries — from [[Agatha Graves]]'s shop. Roselake has no Catholic church, making Agatha one of the only local sources for such items.

On a recent visit, Clara came in looking visibly distressed: bags under her eyes, a wild and twitchy manner. Agatha noticed but did not press. This visit aligns with the Marshes' escalating fear and their recent shift from new age paraphernalia toward Catholic imagery.

Agatha now knows the Marsh name from Thomas McMullin's Weeping House case file, and is aware of Clara's state. Whether she chooses to follow up on this connection is an open question.

## Open Threads

- The Marshes are genuinely scared. Even if the mundane truth is explained to them (corroded galvanized plumbing), they may not believe it. People who have decided their house is infested by a demon do not easily accept "it's just pipes."
- Their recent turn toward Catholicism and demonology could attract attention — either from well-meaning church members or from less well-meaning entities that feed on religious terror.
- Despite the mundane truth of their case, their fear is real, and concentrated fear has consequences in Roselake.
