# PART ONE — Thomas's File

_*This case is newer than the others, but equally if not more well developed than the others; the possibility of a demonic incursion has been pulling Thomas from other cases to focus on this one. The only one he spends more time & effort on is the Skinwalker case.*



## Thomas's Theory

Thomas has been hearing from **Reuben and Clara Marsh**, a couple in their late 50s who live in one of the older houses on the south end of town. The Marshes are deeply superstitious people — their home is filled with tarot cards, crystals, books on chakra alignment, and a dedicated seance room from their years in the new age movement. More recently, the house has been overtaken by Catholic paraphernalia — crosses on every wall, books on demonology and spiritual warfare, a rosary over the bedroom mirror, and holy water by the front door. (See [[The Marsh Couple]] for details on their background and superstitions.)

The Marshes have been complaining to anyone who will listen — the diner, the church, the general store — about increasingly disturbing events in their home. Thomas believes their house may be haunted or infested by something unnatural.

## What the Marshes Have Reported

- **Banging sounds at night.** Deep, resonant thuds from inside the walls, sometimes in rhythmic bursts, sometimes a single loud crack. They come and go. Reuben says it sounds "like someone pounding to get out."
- **Blood from the taps.** The kitchen sink and bathtub have intermittently produced what Clara insists is blood. She is unwavering on this point — it is not rust, it is not discolored water, it is *blood.* It doesn't always happen, which makes it worse. Sometimes it runs clear for days, then suddenly comes out thick and red, without any discernible pattern. Clara has saved samples in drinking glasses to show Thomas. She cannot explain why there is no pattern to it, and this lack of pattern frightens her more than the blood itself.
- **Cold spots.** Certain rooms in the house — particularly the upstairs hallway and the back bedroom — are noticeably, inexplicably colder than the rest of the house, even with the heat running. Clara says she can feel drafts coming from the walls themselves.
- **A sulfurous smell.** An intermittent rotten-egg smell that comes and goes, strongest in the bathroom and kitchen. It's not constant — it flares up for a few hours, then disappears for days. The Marshes have checked for gas leaks and found nothing. When they describe this smell, Reuben and Clara grow nervous, hesitant — but they speak with conviction. The sulfur, they say, is the sign. This is not a departed human soul. This is not a ghost. What is in their house is **demonic and hellish in nature.** Clara clutches the rosary she now wears around her neck when she says this. Reuben nods, jaw tight. They are anxious, frightened people who have latched onto this interpretation with the desperate grip of those who need their fear to have a name.
- **A stain on the ceiling.** A yellowish-brown stain has been slowly spreading across the ceiling of the upstairs hallway. It appeared about two months ago and has been growing. It's damp to the touch. Clara says it looks like the house is "sweating." Thomas checked the rooftop and found no source for a leak — and the Marshes have seized on this. If Thomas found no leak on the roof, then there *is* no leak. The stain cannot be water. Thomas McMullin, who is not a plumber, simply went along with this reasoning at the time, and now the Marshes treat it as established fact: the house is weeping something that isn't water, from a source that doesn't exist, and that means something unholy is inside it.

Thomas visited the house once. He confirmed the cold spots and the ceiling stain. He did not witness the blood from the taps or the banging during his visit, but he found the atmosphere of the house deeply unsettling — heavy, damp, and oppressive.

## What Thomas Thinks

Thomas suspects the house is haunted by something, but he's not sure what it is, what its nature is, what its cause is, or what it wants. He initially believed that equating the sulfurous smell with a demon was "jumping to conclusions" — but the Marshes' conviction is wearing on him. The blood from the taps, the stain that keeps spreading, the sulfur, the cold — it does fit the profile of a demonic presence, and Thomas is slowly, reluctantly beginning to accept that the Marshes may be right. He has been spending more time on this case than almost any other, pulled away from other investigations by the weight of what a confirmed demonic incursion would mean.

# PART TWO — What's Actually Happening

_The mundane truth. There is nothing supernatural about the Marsh house._

## The Pipes

The Marsh house was built in the early 1900s and still has its original galvanized steel plumbing. The pipes are corroding from the inside out. This single cause explains every symptom:

**Banging sounds:** Water hammer — caused by pressure fluctuations in corroding pipes with mineral buildup and partially blocked flow. When a faucet is turned off or the water heater cycles, the sudden change in pressure sends shockwaves through the pipe system, producing loud banging or knocking sounds. The irregular rhythm is caused by air pockets trapped in the deteriorating pipes.

**Blood from the taps:** Iron oxide (rust) from the corroding galvanized pipe interiors. It accumulates in the pipes and is released intermittently when water pressure shifts, when pipes are jostled by the water hammer, or after periods of low use (overnight, for example). The intermittent nature is characteristic of galvanized pipe corrosion — the rust builds up, dislodges, runs red for a while, then clears until the next buildup breaks loose. To a superstitious couple predisposed to belief in the supernatural, rust-red water from the taps is blood — and Clara has described it as such without hesitation.

**Cold spots:** The pipe corrosion has created small gaps and pinhole leaks in the walls, particularly at pipe joints. These allow outside air to enter the wall cavities and seep through into the rooms. The upstairs hallway and back bedroom are coldest because that's where the worst corrosion is — the main supply lines run through those walls.

**Sulfurous smell:** Hydrogen sulfide gas produced by sulfate-reducing bacteria thriving in the stagnant, mineral-rich water inside the corroding pipes. This is common in old galvanized systems, especially in houses with well water or older municipal connections. The smell comes and goes because the bacteria produce gas in cycles and the gas escapes through the same pinhole leaks causing the cold drafts.

**Ceiling stain:** A slow leak from a corroded pipe joint in the ceiling above the upstairs hallway. The yellowish-brown color is from dissolved minerals and rust in the leaking water. It's damp because it's an active, ongoing leak — the pipe is dripping slowly into the ceiling cavity. Thomas checked the roof and found no leak from above — because the leak isn't from the roof. It's from the pipes inside the ceiling. But Thomas is not a plumber, and the Marshes have taken his rooftop inspection as proof that the stain has no physical cause.

## Why It Looks Scary

Everything about this situation is mundane, but the combination — the knocking, the blood-red water, the cold, the smell, the spreading stain — creates an overwhelmingly creepy atmosphere, especially for a deeply superstitious couple who have spent their lives believing in the supernatural. The Marshes are on the older end of people who joined the new age movement in the 1960s, and they have recently shifted from crystals and chakras to crosses and demonology. They are primed to interpret these symptoms as demonic, and every failed attempt at explanation only deepens their conviction.

A plumber could diagnose this in an afternoon. The fix is a full replumb — expensive but straightforward.

## Value as a Red Herring

This case serves to eat up player time and resources if they pursue it, but it also teaches them an important lesson: not everything in Roselake is supernatural. Some things are just old and broken. This should make them better investigators for the real cases — or make them second-guess themselves at a critical moment.
