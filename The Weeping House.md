# The Case of the Weeping House

---

# PART ONE — Thomas's File

_What Thomas McMullin has gathered. This is a newer case in his collection — less developed than the others, but alarming enough that he's started a folder._

## Thomas's Theory

Thomas has been hearing from **Reuben and Clara Marsh**, a couple in their late 50s who live in one of the older houses on the south end of town. The Marshes have been complaining to anyone who will listen — the diner, the church, the general store — about increasingly disturbing events in their home. Thomas believes their house may be haunted or infested by something unnatural.

## What the Marshes Have Reported

- **Banging sounds at night.** Deep, resonant thuds from inside the walls, sometimes in rhythmic bursts, sometimes a single loud crack. They come and go. Reuben says it sounds "like someone pounding to get out."
- **Reddish-brown water.** The kitchen sink and bathtub have intermittently produced water that is rust-colored — anywhere from light orange to a deep brownish-red. Clara says it looks like watered-down blood. It doesn't always happen, which makes it worse. Sometimes it runs clear for days, then suddenly comes out red.
- **Cold spots.** Certain rooms in the house — particularly the upstairs hallway and the back bedroom — are noticeably, inexplicably colder than the rest of the house, even with the heat running. Clara says she can feel drafts coming from the walls themselves.
- **A sulfurous smell.** An intermittent rotten-egg smell that comes and goes, strongest in the bathroom and kitchen. It's not constant — it flares up for a few hours, then disappears for days. The Marshes have checked for gas leaks and found nothing.
- **A stain on the ceiling.** A yellowish-brown stain has been slowly spreading across the ceiling of the upstairs hallway. It appeared about two months ago and has been growing. It's damp to the touch. Clara says it looks like the house is "sweating."

Thomas visited the house once. He confirmed the cold spots and the ceiling stain. He did not witness the red water or the banging during his visit, but he found the atmosphere of the house deeply unsettling — heavy, damp, and oppressive.

## What Thomas Thinks

Thomas suspects the house is either haunted by something that is slowly manifesting through the building itself — bleeding through the walls, breathing through the pipes — or that the house is "sick" in some supernatural sense, as if the structure itself has been corrupted. He's considering the possibility that something is buried beneath the house.

# PART TWO — What's Actually Happening

_The mundane truth. There is nothing supernatural about the Marsh house._

## The Pipes

The Marsh house was built in the early 1900s and still has its original galvanized steel plumbing. The pipes are corroding from the inside out. This single cause explains every symptom:

**Banging sounds:** Water hammer — caused by pressure fluctuations in corroding pipes with mineral buildup and partially blocked flow. When a faucet is turned off or the water heater cycles, the sudden change in pressure sends shockwaves through the pipe system, producing loud banging or knocking sounds. The irregular rhythm is caused by air pockets trapped in the deteriorating pipes.

**Reddish-brown water:** Iron oxide (rust) from the corroding galvanized pipe interiors. It accumulates in the pipes and is released intermittently when water pressure shifts, when pipes are jostled by the water hammer, or after periods of low use (overnight, for example). The intermittent nature is characteristic of galvanized pipe corrosion — the rust builds up, dislodges, runs red for a while, then clears until the next buildup breaks loose.

**Cold spots:** The pipe corrosion has created small gaps and pinhole leaks in the walls, particularly at pipe joints. These allow outside air to enter the wall cavities and seep through into the rooms. The upstairs hallway and back bedroom are coldest because that's where the worst corrosion is — the main supply lines run through those walls.

**Sulfurous smell:** Hydrogen sulfide gas produced by sulfate-reducing bacteria thriving in the stagnant, mineral-rich water inside the corroding pipes. This is common in old galvanized systems, especially in houses with well water or older municipal connections. The smell comes and goes because the bacteria produce gas in cycles and the gas escapes through the same pinhole leaks causing the cold drafts.

**Ceiling stain:** A slow leak from a corroded pipe joint in the ceiling above the upstairs hallway. The yellowish-brown color is from dissolved minerals and rust in the leaking water. It's damp because it's an active, ongoing leak — the pipe is dripping slowly into the ceiling cavity.

## Why It Looks Scary

Everything about this situation is mundane, but the combination — the knocking, the red water, the cold, the smell, the spreading stain — creates an overwhelmingly creepy atmosphere, especially for people who aren't plumbers. The Marshes are a couple in their late 50s who have no reason to understand galvanized pipe failure, and the symptoms all appeared gradually over months as the corrosion worsened, making it feel like an escalation.

A plumber could diagnose this in an afternoon. The fix is a full replumb — expensive but straightforward.

## Value as a Red Herring

This case serves to eat up player time and resources if they pursue it, but it also teaches them an important lesson: not everything in Roselake is supernatural. Some things are just old and broken. This should make them better investigators for the real cases — or make them second-guess themselves at a critical moment.

## Open Threads

- How long do the players spend on this before figuring it out? A successful investigation, a plumber NPC, or even just turning on a faucet and watching rust water come out could crack it.
- Reuben and Clara are genuinely scared. Even after the mundane explanation, they may need convincing — or they may not believe it. People who've decided their house is haunted don't always accept "it's just pipes."
- Is there anything actually unusual buried under or near the house that could serve as a secondary hook, or is this purely a dead end?
