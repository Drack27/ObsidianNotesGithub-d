# Session 6

**Date (in-game):** March 18 – April 3, 1981
**Player Characters:** [[Matthew Davis]]
**Players:** Tucker (Matthew)

---

## Events

### Waking Up in the Fire Station (March 18, 1981)

[[Matthew Davis]] came to consciousness in the [[Roselake Fire Station]]'s makeshift medical room under the buzzing of a fluorescent light — a brick-walled room with a couple of cots, a first-aid workstation, and a lamp on a jointed arm in the corner. His arm was wrapped and splinted, bandages covered most of his body, and a massive torso brace held his spine in position. He could not feel his legs. The memory of falling from the radio tower came back in pieces.

He called for help. After a few terrifying moments of silence, the older female dispatcher from [[Session 3]] and [[Session 5]] — gruff, gravelly-voiced, cigarette perpetually in hand — waddled in and began tending to his wounds. She spoke plainly: he was healing, they would keep him a couple of days, and then he would need to go home. She asked if there was anyone who could look after him. There wasn't.

She mentioned Reverend Detty: she had gone looking for him after work and couldn't find him anywhere. Not at his house. Not at the church. Not at Tony's. As far as she knew, he was gone.

A young, sandy-haired man in his late teens or early twenties — **Steven** — helped with evening meals and care.

### Days of Recovery (March 18–22, 1981)

Matthew spent approximately five days in the fire station's medical room, healing under the dispatcher's care (rolling 1d3 HP each day). Time stretched and compressed. Fever dreams came and went.

One night, Matthew dreamed of an infinite grocery store aisle — shelves of unrecognizable brands, text that slipped from his mind when he tried to read it. [[Wendy Simons]], gray-skinned and ramrod-straight, told him flatly that *the store is closed.* Further down the aisle, [[Thomas McMullin]] drifted with a black tendril emerging from his back. When Matthew turned and walked forward, he fell — and saw flashes: a woman with crystals, a generator, a dark mountain, an eye. And something he couldn't see at all, which frightened him more than any of the things he could.

As the days passed, he regained full hit points. He was cleared of his major wound status, though the paralysis remained.

On approximately day three, [[Fire Chief Bill]] and a lanky firefighter named **Leonard** arrived with a kludged-together walker — built to work with Matthew's back brace — for use in physical therapy. Over two days, they walked him back and forth across a room. The results were poor. Matthew could scoot his way across one room before the pain forced him to stop. The dispatcher's verdict: he could be discharged, but would be almost entirely bedbound at home.

### Transfer Home (March 23, 1981)

Matthew was loaded into the fire truck and taken home. On arrival, the firefighters asked him to describe his house so they could get him situated:

- **Single-story house** with a garage for his car
- **Kitchen**, two bedrooms (one used as a study), and a **bathroom off the primary bedroom** — accessible without crossing the house
- A window beside his bed, facing the backyard

Bill and Leonard wheeled him into the bedroom, unhooked him from the walker apparatus, brought in a supply of **MREs** (sourced from [[Joe's Superstore]]), and promised to return each day for physical therapy. They set his expected recovery end date at **April 13, 1981** — three weeks out — and warned him not to push himself. Then they locked the door and left.

Matthew's move rate was dropped to zero. He was alone, crippled, in his own home.

That night, loud and urgent knocking at the front door woke him in the dark. Matthew reached over and opened his bedroom window. He called out that he couldn't come to the door. The knocking stopped immediately. No one responded. Nothing further happened.

### Physical Therapy and the Revelation (~March 24–27, 1981)

The next morning, the day was quiet. Bill came in the evening — knocking at the window, retrieving the key from under the mat — and walked Matthew back and forth across the room as physical therapy. It was a disaster. Bill is a firefighter, not a physical therapist, and the session ended in stumbling and wall-banging. Bill, doing his best, wished Matthew luck and left.

The following day, a full hard success on both Constitution and Dexterity reduced Matthew's sentence by one day — **April 12, 1981** was now the target date.

Several days of similar sessions followed: Bill (and occasionally other firefighters) coming each morning or afternoon, walking Matthew back and forth, the routine settling into something almost normal.

Then, on approximately **March 27**, Bill didn't come. Matthew called the fire station that evening and spoke to Stephen, who said Bill wasn't on the night shift and offered to have him call the next morning.

The following morning, Bill called very early — and his voice was strange. He sounded confused. He didn't recognize Matthew's name. When Matthew described his situation — the injury, the fire station, the medical care, the physical therapy — Bill was silent for a long moment.

When Bill arrived with Leonard, they gave Matthew a thorough look-over. His physical condition matched everything he'd described. But both men were firm: **neither of them had treated Matthew. Neither had arranged the home discharge. Neither had been coming to his house.**

They had no memory of any of it.

Bill's working theory was neurological damage from the spinal injury — memory inconsistencies that would need to be evaluated by a specialist from Omaha. He declined to file a police report. He mentioned he had spoken with **Susan** (the dispatcher) and that she would keep an eye on things. He offered to continue the physical therapy regardless, taking Matthew's account in good faith even if he couldn't explain it. He and Leonard left looking bewildered and concerned.

What actually happened in those missing days — who brought Matthew home, who tended to him, who was walking him through his recovery — is unknown. Given what came later, it appears likely that **[[Jonathan Oesch]]** had been watching and caring for Matthew, intending to keep him alive for his own purposes.

### Reverend Detty Confirmed Missing (March 29, 1981)

On Sunday March 29, the dispatcher ([[Shawna]]) came with Bill for a medical check-in — behaving, as Bill had, as if it were her first visit since the accident. She made pointed remarks about Reverend Detty's "wild theories" and for nearly getting Matthew killed. She also noted that **Detty had not appeared at Sunday church service**, and that the congregation had been left confused and without a pastor for the morning. He remained unaccounted for.

### Isolation and Disconnection (March 30 – April 2, 1981)

Beginning March 30, the firefighters stopped coming. No Bill. No Leonard. No one.

Matthew called the fire station on March 31 — dead tone. Same on April 1 and April 2. He lost **1 sanity point** as the isolation and the strangeness of the past weeks began to compound. Time was losing its edges.

### First Contact: Jonathan Oesch (Night of April 2, 1981)

Lying awake in the dark on April 2, Matthew heard knocking at his front door — louder and rougher than Bill's usual knock. When he didn't respond, a flashlight swept through the window behind his bed. A silhouetted figure shone it directly into Matthew's face, then killed the light and spoke from the dark:

*"No one will remember you... Out here, it's like space. No one can hear you scream... I will be back. No one will remember you, except for me."*

The voice — male, confident, almost philosophical — was followed by footsteps crunching away through the backyard. This was **[[Jonathan Oesch]]**, making his first direct contact with a player character.

### The Attack (Night of April 3, 1981)

Matthew spent April 3 doing self-directed physical therapy — pushing himself alone with mixed results.

That night, knocking at the bedroom window. Matthew opened the blinds and glimpsed a hand pull back. He opened the window and called out.

The same voice answered — taunting, asking whether Matthew was alone, whether the door was unlocked. When Matthew refused, there was a brief pause, followed by a metallic crash and bang: the front door being forced. Boots crossed the living room floor slowly, deliberately. The bedroom door opened.

A massive silhouette stood in the dark — **approximately six and a half feet tall**, wide-shouldered.

Matthew drew his revolver and fired at point-blank range. He hit — wounding the figure's arm. The figure barely slowed. He crossed the room and wrestled the gun from Matthew's grip, passing it awkwardly to his other hand and pointing it at Matthew's head.

In the subsequent muzzle flashes: **shaggy long blonde hair**, wild eyes, a muscular human frame. This was Jonathan Oesch.

The fight that followed moved from the bedroom into the living room:

- Matthew shattered his lamp across Jonathan's head, sending him stumbling into the doorway.
- Matthew stood up from his bed on adrenaline and a successful Constitution roll.
- Jonathan began **chanting in Latin** — words Matthew, fluent in Latin as an archaeologist, heard but could not focus on amid the chaos. A ritual invocation.
- Jonathan declared: *"I wanted to keep you around, but I can change my method."*
- Jonathan pistol-whipped Matthew across the face (**4 damage**).
- Matthew shut the bedroom door; Jonathan shot through it — the bullet splintered the door but missed.
- Matthew slashed at Jonathan with a shard of the broken lamp as the door came open again.
- Jonathan fired the revolver — the bullet grazed Matthew's shoulder (**3 damage**, not a major wound).
- Jonathan pistol-whipped Matthew a second time (**4 damage**).
- Jonathan declared: *"No one will remember your name. He'll be just like me."* The gun clicked empty.
- Matthew, at the edge of consciousness, swung one last time — and took a punch to the face (**4 damage**).

Matthew went to **0 HP** and lost consciousness.

He is now unconscious on the floor of his living room. Jonathan Oesch is in possession of his revolver. Matthew is alive, but completely helpless. What Jonathan does next remains to be seen.

## Significance

This session was a solo session for [[Matthew Davis]], covering approximately two weeks of in-game time as he recovered from his near-fatal injuries at the [[Old Radio Tower]].

The central revelation: **the firefighters have no memory of caring for Matthew** during his recovery and discharge. Someone — almost certainly Jonathan Oesch, based on his later statements about "keeping Matthew around" — was providing care in the interim, ensuring Matthew stayed alive. Jonathan apparently intended to use [[The Catalyst]] on Matthew and add him to his collection of prisoners.

[[Reverend Detty]] has confirmed vanished. He missed Sunday church service on March 29 and has not been seen anywhere in town since the night of the radio tower fall. His whereabouts are unknown.

[[Jonathan Oesch]] has now made direct physical contact with a player character for the first time. He is confirmed as the figure behind the antimemetic killings — and his intent toward Matthew is explicit: he wanted Matthew forgotten and imprisoned, just like Victor Mann and Sally Oesch. He may still accomplish this.

Matthew's revolver is in Jonathan's possession.
