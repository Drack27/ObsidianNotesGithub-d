# Sheriff Hank Thompson

**Age:** Mid 50s
**Residence:** A house near the Sheriff's Office in the town center.
**Occupation:** Sheriff of Roselake.
**Case Connections:** [[Lonely Painting]], [[The Antimemetic Killer]]

---

## Description

Sheriff Hank Thompson is a heavyset man with a weathered face, a salt-and-pepper mustache, and the permanently tired expression of someone who has been doing a thankless job for too long. He wears his badge on a leather vest over a flannel shirt and drives a patrol truck that has seen better decades.

Hank is not corrupt. He is not lazy. He is simply a small-town sheriff who is profoundly, completely out of his depth — and has been for years.

---

## Personality

Hank is pragmatic to a fault. He believes in evidence, procedure, and common sense. When evidence doesn't exist, procedure fails, and common sense offers no explanation, Hank's instinct is to close the case and move on. Not because he doesn't care, but because he genuinely does not know what else to do.

He is frustrated by his own limitations and privately ashamed of the cold cases piling up on his desk. The Peabody house — three families, twelve people, zero answers — haunts him. He has investigated that farmhouse three times and found nothing three times. Each failure chips away at him a little more.

He is not hostile to outside help, but he is defensive. If the players approach him with respect, he will cooperate. If they approach him with accusations, he will shut down.

---

## Investigative Record

**The Peabody House (The Lonely Painting):** Three separate investigations, three separate failures. No evidence of forced entry, no blood, no bodies. Cars in the driveway, food in the fridge. Hank has no theory and no leads. He privately considers the Peabody house the biggest failure of his career.

**Bill Donahue's Murder:** A cursory investigation at best. Bill was a truck driver with no local ties — a man who passed through. Hank took a report, canvassed the area, found nothing, and let it go. One newspaper article. No follow-up. Thomas McMullin considers this deeply suspicious; the truth is more mundane. Hank simply didn't have the resources or the pressure to pursue it further, and the ambient influence of The Forgotten may have further dulled his interest.

**Victor Mann & Sally Oesch:** Hank has no memory of either person. The Catalyst has erased them from his mind along with everyone else in town. If confronted with newspaper evidence of their existence, he would be confused and unsettled — but not immediately suspicious. People move away. Records get lost. That's his first instinct.

---

## Relationship with Thomas McMullin

Hank knew Thomas as the librarian — quiet, bookish, harmless. He was not aware of the extent of Thomas's investigations. If told that Thomas had been compiling case files on supernatural activity in Roselake, Hank would be skeptical but not dismissive. He's too tired to dismiss anything outright anymore.
