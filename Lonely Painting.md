# The Case of the Peabody House

## The Hook

Thomas McMullin has heard talk at the diner about the old Peabody farmhouse, about 4 miles southwest of town. Three full families have moved into the house in the past 5 years, and all three have gone missing. The Sheriff has come up empty-handed every single time — no evidence of foul play, or at best limited and inconclusive evidence. Thomas can't let it go.

## The House

The Peabody farm was home to the Peabody family for generations — a big multigenerational household of around 12 people at its peak. Over the years, the family slowly dwindled and moved away. The last one to leave did so in 1968, leaving only the patriarch, **Elias Peabody**, behind. Old Mr. Peabody was well-known in town; he served as the local pastor for a time. He lived alone in the house until his death roughly 5 years ago, after which the property began cycling through new owners.

---

## What Really Happened

### The Painting

After his last family member moved out in 1968, Mr. Peabody had several years for the isolation to warp his mind. He tried to spend time in town, but he couldn't always manage it, and he didn't want to leave his home. Eventually, he poured every ounce of his agonizing loneliness into a single painting: a huge gray landscape depicting a massive swath of cornfields under a heavy, overcast sky, with a single dead tree growing out of the center of the frame — far, far in the distance, far from anyone.

He locked the painting in the attic and felt much better.

What he didn't know was that many others in Roselake felt the same way. That shared, ambient loneliness allowed **the Isolation** to supernaturally touch the painting, imbuing it with power. The painting draws in anyone who lingers near it while feeling lonely — and once it takes them, they're gone.

_(Reference: Magnus Archives, pg. 259 for the "sucking in" mechanic.)_

---

### Family #1 — The Jenkins (Family of 5)

A young family. Their daughter, **Ida Jenkins**, felt bullied at school because of her old-fashioned name — already carrying a seed of isolation. She found the painting while exploring the attic and became obsessed with it, hanging it up in her room. The rest of the family thought it was weird but let it go.

The painting sucked her in.

The family went through the whole ordeal with the Sheriff, who found nothing. Grieving, they left her room exactly as it was — painting included. One by one, the remaining family members were drawn in as well. They each felt an intense loneliness near Ida's room and thought they could alleviate it by spending time where she had spent time. It was the opposite. It only widened the hole. Eventually, all five were gone.

### Family #2 — The Calvins (Family of 3)

Moved in from out of town. Their son, **Isaac**, was an edgelord who thought the painting fit his metal/emo aesthetic. He decorated the room black and leaned hard into the "nobody understands me" vibe — making him a perfect target. He was sucked in. The pattern of familial grief experienced by his parents repeated: they lingered near the painting, and it took them too.

### Family #3 — The Jacksons (Family of 4)

An eccentric, artistic family. The father **David** was a writer, the mother **Grace** a painter, the daughter **Emily** a musician, and the daughter **Sara** a sculptor. Unlike the previous families, they hung the painting in the **main living area**. They liked its melancholy quality. Each one of them jockeyed for time to spend alone with it.

They were all eventually sucked in.

Their dog, **Filmore**, was found half-starved about 3 weeks ago, having survived on scraps for a full week on his own. Someone only checked on the family because Emily and Sara's school friends wondered where they were.

---

## Thomas's File

Thomas doesn't know what's wrong with the house. He's been photographing it from every angle and has laid out the full public timeline of the three families — their arrivals, the disappearances, the fruitless Sheriff investigations — but that's as far as he's gotten. He has no theory about the cause. Just the pattern, and the certainty that something is very wrong.
