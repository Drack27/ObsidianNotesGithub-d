# PLAYER HANDOUT — The Case of the Missing Missing

## GM Note: The Manila Folder

_This folder is one of the thicker ones in Thomas's collection, held shut with a rubber band that has been doubled over twice. The tab is labeled "THE MISSING MISSING" in black marker. The word "MISSING" is written twice on purpose — Thomas has underlined both. Inside, the folder is organized with newspaper clippings on the left side and Thomas's handwritten notes on the right. A large red question mark has been drawn on the inside front cover in marker._

---

## Left Side — The Newspaper Clippings

_The left pocket of the folder contains a stack of clippings from the Roselake Register, sorted chronologically. Each one has been carefully cut out and has handwritten annotations in the margins. Describe the following to the players:_

---

### CLIPPING 1 — Community Cleanup Day Photo

A photograph from the Register showing a group of about a dozen volunteers in work gloves standing in front of a pile of brush and debris at what looks like a vacant lot. The caption lists names. Thomas has circled one: **VICTOR MANN.** The man in the photo is unremarkable — middle-aged, thin, wearing a plaid shirt, holding a rake. He's smiling slightly.

Thomas has written in the margin: **"Ask anyone in this photo about him. They don't know who he is."**

---

### CLIPPING 2 — School Fall Festival Announcement

A short piece from the Register announcing the annual Roselake School Fall Festival. It lists organizing volunteers and thanks them by name. **VICTOR MANN, HEAD CUSTODIAN** is listed among them.

Thomas's margin note: **"The school doesn't have records of employing a Victor Mann. I checked."**

---

### CLIPPING 3 — Church Directory Excerpt

A photocopied page from what appears to be a First Methodist Church directory or bulletin. Several names and addresses are listed. Thomas has circled **VICTOR MANN** and, on a separate line further down, **SALLY OESCH.**

Thomas's margin note: **"Both in the same directory. Both ghosts now."**

---

### CLIPPING 4 — Volunteer Appreciation Blurb

A brief Register piece, maybe three column inches, recognizing volunteers at a holiday charity drive. **SALLY OESCH** is mentioned by name as having helped organize donations. A small, slightly blurry photo accompanies the article — a woman in her 30s with short hair, handing a box to someone off-frame.

Thomas's margin note: **"She was HERE. She DID things. She was part of this town. And now her own brother acts like she never existed."**

---

### CLIPPING 5 — Brief Mention: Bill Donahue

A very short Register piece — barely a blurb — noting that a body was discovered on the outskirts of town. The deceased is identified as **BILL DONAHUE**, described as a truck driver who regularly passed through Roselake. The article states that the Sheriff's office is investigating.

Thomas has drawn a thick box around the entire clipping and written: **"This is the ONLY article about this. A man was found DEAD and there was ONE article and NO follow-up. No arrest. No suspect. Nothing."**

---

### CLIPPING 6 — Real Estate Listing

A short classified-style listing from the Register for a house for sale. The address matches the one Thomas has written down for Victor Mann in his notes. There is no mention of Victor by name.

Thomas's margin note: **"Who put this house up for sale? Victor? He's gone. His family? Nobody remembers his family. The COUNTY? On what grounds?"**

## Right Side — Thomas's Handwritten Notes

_The right pocket of the folder contains several pages of lined notebook paper covered in Thomas's handwriting. The notes are written in blue and black ink at different times, with some sections underlined or starred._

---

### Page 1 — "WHAT I KNOW"

> **THE MISSING MISSING — CASE NOTES** _T. McMullin_
> 
> Three people. All connected to this town. All gone. And nobody — NOBODY — remembers them.
> 
> I'm not talking about "oh, I haven't seen them in a while." I'm talking about _total erasure._ I say their names and people look at me like I'm speaking another language. I show them the newspaper clippings — photos of these people STANDING NEXT TO THEM — and they shrug. "I don't know who that is." "Must be someone from out of town."
> 
> These aren't strangers. These were members of this community.

---

### Page 2 — The Three

> **VICTOR MANN**
> 
> - Janitor at Roselake School. Multiple newspaper mentions. Fall festival, cleanup days, church directory.
> - Lived alone. No wife or kids that I can find.
> - Active in community but... now that I think about it, I can't find evidence of close friends. No wedding announcements, no "Victor and so-and-so" at a dinner party. Just Victor, alone, showing up to things.
> - At some point, all trace of him just STOPS. House went up for sale. School has no employment records for him. Nobody at the school — teachers, staff, NOBODY — remembers him.
> - I only found him because I was going through old Registers looking for something else.
> 
> **SALLY OESCH**
> 
> - Lived with her brother Jonathan on the south end of town.
> - Church volunteer. Charity drives. Appeared in the Register a handful of times.
> - Similar profile to Victor — active but not deeply connected. Showed up, helped out, went home.
> - Her brother Jonathan still lives in the house. I asked around about Sally — nothing. Asked at the church — nothing. Nobody remembers a Sally Oesch.
> - I haven't talked to Jonathan directly. Something about him makes me uneasy and I don't know what.
> 
> **BILL DONAHUE**
> 
> - Truck driver. Regular route through Roselake. Not a local but a familiar face.
> - Least social of the regular drivers — didn't hang around the diner or make small talk. Just passed through.
> - Found DEAD on the outskirts of town. One newspaper article. No follow-up. No arrest.
> - Sheriff Thompson's investigation: nonexistent, as far as I can tell.
> - Bill's death lines up with the timeline. Right around when Victor and Sally stopped appearing in public records, when their houses went on the market, when the town started forgetting them.
> - Coincidence? I don't buy coincidences anymore.

---

### Page 3 — "WHAT DOESN'T ADD UP"

> Things that bother me:
> 
> 1. This isn't normal forgetting. People forget acquaintances all the time. But this is EVERYONE forgetting EVERYTHING about these people, all at once. Exposed photos, printed names, doesn't matter. They look at the evidence and feel nothing.
>     
> 2. Victor and Sally have the same profile — active in the community, visible in public, but no deep personal bonds. No close friends, no family nearby (except Jonathan, in Sally's case). Is that a coincidence or a pattern?
>     
> 3. Bill Donahue is the odd one out. He didn't live here. He was passing through. But he's DEAD, and the other two are just GONE. What connects a murdered truck driver to two forgotten locals?
>     
> 4. The Sheriff doesn't care. About any of it. Three people vanished from the public record and a man is dead and he hasn't lifted a finger. Either he's in on it or he's been made to forget too.
>     
> 5. Jonathan Oesch is still here. His sister is gone — or rather, was never here, according to everyone — and he's just living his life. Is he a victim? Is he involved? Why does he make me nervous?
>     

---

### Page 4 — "THEORIES (God Help Me)"

> I keep coming back to government involvement. Some kind of program. Experimental. Maybe chemical — something in the water, something airborne, something targeted.
> 
> Why these three? Maybe they saw something they shouldn't have. Maybe they were selected. Victor worked at the SCHOOL — access to the building after hours. Sally volunteered everywhere — maybe she overheard something. Bill was a truck driver — maybe he was transporting something.
> 
> Or maybe it's simpler than that. Maybe someone in this town has the ability to make people disappear — not just physically, but from MEMORY — and they're testing it. Perfecting it. And Victor, Sally, and Bill were easy targets because nobody would fight hard enough to remember them.
> 
> That last one keeps me up at night.
